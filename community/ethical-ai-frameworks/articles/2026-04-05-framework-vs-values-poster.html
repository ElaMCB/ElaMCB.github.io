A Framework Is Not a Values Poster: What Teams Should Document

If it does not change a ticket, a test, or a sign-off, it is wallpaper. Below is a sharper way to separate slogans from something auditors—and your own engineers—can actually trace.

“We take ethics seriously” is not a framework. A framework is what happens the Tuesday after a model does something embarrassing in production.

The wallpaper test

Ask one question in your next staff meeting: name the last three product decisions where this document changed what someone shipped. If the room goes quiet, you do not have a framework yet—you have a comms asset. That is not shameful; most teams start there. The fix is to bolt process to paper.

Ethical AI work fails in the wild for boring reasons: nobody owns the decision, evidence lives in Slack threads, and “we discussed it” does not survive a regulator or an angry customer thread six months later. Documentation that matters is tied to versioned artifacts: model IDs, prompts, retrieval configs, evaluation runs, and who signed what.

Poster vs framework (same wall, different job)

Values poster Working framework
Lists virtues (fairness, transparency) Names who decides fairness for this product and what artifact proves it was considered
Hangs in the lobby or PDF Lives next to the release checklist, CI job, or ticket template engineers already open
Stable for years Versioned when data, model, jurisdiction, or use case shifts—because the risk surface moved
Hard to falsify (“we care”) Easy to audit: empty cell = gap you can see in a spreadsheet

Three artifacts that make it real

A useful ethical AI framework ties abstract principles to decisions someone actually makes: who approves a new model or feature, what evidence they need, and what happens when a check fails. Without those hooks, “be fair” and “respect privacy” are slogans, not engineering constraints.

1
Intake in one sentence. What changed—a fine-tune, a new tool, a data source, a jurisdiction? If you cannot state it without jargon, the review will wander.
2
Named owners, not “the org.” One accountable approver for ship/no-ship, one deputy, one escalation path. “Legal will weigh in” is not a name.
3
Reviews you can schedule. Bias and representativeness, privacy and retention, safety and misuse, human oversight when the model acts—the mix depends on your domain, but the calendar slot has to exist.
4
Exit criteria for production. What must be true to merge or deploy: eval thresholds, red-team pass, DPIA on file, customer comms—pick what matches your risk, write it as checkboxes.

That one-page decision map is your framework in practice; the glossy diagram is optional. If you want it to feel more “designed,” put the diagram beside the checklist, not instead of it.

Why posters still appear (and how to use them)

Leadership often asks for visible ethics commitments before process exists. That is fine as long as you file the poster next to a living checklist in Jira, Asana, or your release tool. The LinkedIn audience for ethical AI frameworks usually wants both: language they can rally around and mechanics they can audit.

Practical pairing: keep the poster for all-hands and onboarding; link from it to a single “how we ship AI” Confluence or Notion page that holds the decision map and the last three run IDs. External readers then see culture and a trail.

Make the next retro uncomfortable (in a good way)

Pick one shipped AI feature from the last quarter. Reconstruct the story without heroics: data sources, prompts, evals, incidents, and who said yes. If that takes more than thirty minutes of archaeology, your next framework iteration should focus on logging and ownership, not adding another principle.

Quick audit you can run this week

  • We can point to the ticket or doc where ethics review happened for the last release.
  • We store model or deployment identifiers next to that decision.
  • We know what we would show Legal or Security if asked tomorrow—not screenshots of chat.
  • Someone is named for updating the map when the product crosses a new country or modality.

Where this breaks down (honestly)

Small teams will skip steps when deadlines bite—that is why the map must be short and live where work already happens. Enterprise teams will over-process—combat that by time-boxing reviews and publishing “good enough for this release” criteria. Neither problem is solved by a prettier poster; both get better when the map is embarrassingly concrete.

Takeaway: If your ethics doc never forces a date, a name, or an artifact into the record, treat it as marketing until you wire those three in. Then you have something worth linking from this series—and worth posting to your group with confidence.
